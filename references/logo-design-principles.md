# Logo Design Principles

Use this reference before logo strategy, generation, review, or refinement.
Treat legal distinctiveness, recognition, reproduction, and product fit as
separate tests. Passing one never implies passing the others.

## 1. Evidence Hierarchy

Use evidence in this order:

1. Product behavior, buyer need, name, and business strategy.
2. Existing brand recognition and real consumption surfaces.
3. Trademark and category collision research.
4. Durable perception, typography, reproduction, and accessibility principles.
5. Current cultural and design trends.
6. Personal taste and visual references.

Never reverse this order. A trend image cannot define the brand concept.

## 2. Durable Industry Consensus

### Source identification before description

A mark exists to identify source, not explain the entire product. The USPTO
distinguishes strong, source-identifying marks from weak descriptive or generic
ones. Apply the same principle visually: avoid category nouns rendered as icons.
A cloud company does not need a cloud; an AI product does not need a sparkle,
brain, circuit, robot, chat bubble, or gradient orb.

Prefer a suggestive relationship over a literal diagram. The viewer may discover
meaning, but recognition must not depend on reading an explanatory paragraph.

### Distinctive, not merely different

Difference is any variation. Distinctiveness is a stable, recallable invariant.
A candidate needs one ownable construction event, such as a proprietary cut,
counterform, terminal, proportion, rhythm, or transformation rule. Adding more
parts, colors, effects, or metaphors does not increase distinctiveness.

### Simple enough to remember, specific enough to own

Reject both extremes:

- generic primitives with no proprietary relationship;
- over-described diagrams that collapse when reduced.

Use the memory redraw test: after a short viewing, can someone reproduce the
defining relationship rather than only name a generic shape?

### One dominant idea

A core mark should have one dominant perceptual event. Secondary details may
support it but must not compete. If the explanation uses three or more independent
metaphors, return to strategy.

### Optical correction over mathematical purity

Equal numbers do not guarantee equal perception. Correct apparent mass, spacing,
curve tension, overshoot, counters, and symbol/wordmark balance by eye. Preserve a
construction logic, but do not worship a grid.

### Negative space must remain intentional

Counterforms are structural material. Check whether they close, merge, or create
unwanted letters and objects at small sizes. Negative space that works only at
presentation scale is decoration, not identity.

### Typography is part of the mark

Do not attach a default geometric sans to a generated symbol. Establish a shared
logic between symbol and wordmark through width, stress, corner behavior, cuts,
terminals, counters, or rhythm. Customize selectively; novelty in every letter
reduces legibility.

## 3. Product-Derived Concept

Before form exploration, write:

1. The buyer sentence: what it is, for whom, and why it is credible.
2. One product mechanism competitors cannot claim in the same combination.
3. The user state change before and after the product.
4. Two tensions or relationships in that mechanism.
5. A list of category cliches and misleading readings.

Translate relationships into formal operations: join, split, interrupt, offset,
phase, fold, overlap, crop, contain, attenuate, register, return, or counterform.
Do not translate product nouns directly into pictograms.

The concept passes only when removing the brand name still leaves a defensible
reason that the form belongs to this product rather than any company in its sector.

## 4. Form Construction

Explore at least three structurally different families. Each family must declare:

- one invariant operation;
- one dominant silhouette;
- one intentional counterform;
- one corner/terminal rule;
- one collision blacklist;
- one small-size simplification rule.

Generate symbol and wordmark candidates on separate branches and one candidate per image. Never ask a model for a combined lockup, logo sheet or board;
multi-logo outputs hide inconsistency and make selection look more rigorous than it
is.

Start in the intended brand palette when color carries hierarchy or identity.
Separately test one-color reduction. Black-and-white is a validation mode, not a
mandatory creative aesthetic.

## 5. Responsive Logo Systems

A production identity normally includes:

- standalone symbol;
- standalone wordmark;
- primary symbol + wordmark lockup;
- horizontal and vertical lockups;
- one-color, reverse, and constrained-color versions;
- micro mark or simplified small-size version;
- favicon/app-icon treatment where the product actually consumes it.

Approve the standalone symbol and standalone wordmark separately. Build lockups
by composing the two approved masters; do not regenerate them together. This
preserves generation capacity, keeps typography readable, and allows systematic
scale and arrangement combinations.

Do not scale one master mechanically into every slot. At small sizes, remove or
widen detail, enlarge critical counters, and correct apparent stroke weight.

For masked mobile icons, keep essential information inside the platform safe zone
and test multiple masks. For functional UI graphics, maintain at least 3:1 contrast
against adjacent colors under WCAG 2.2 non-text contrast; brand marks used only as
decorative identity are not automatically UI controls, but the same contrast test
is still a useful robustness check.

## 6. Current Trend Signals

Trend reports describe the environment, not the answer.

LogoLounge's 2025 review of more than 30,000 logos reports:

- small screens favor higher contrast, bolder color, and rapidly readable icons;
- motion increasingly functions as an identifier rather than decoration;
- highly chromatic futurist palettes coexist with muted, earthy palettes;
- typography splits between expressive, tactile forms and stark sans wordmarks;
- mascots and human warmth are rising as a response to impersonal systems;
- AI creates excessive option volume, making editing and rejection more important;
- visible form currents include deep controlled cuts, rounded mechanical forms,
  active negative-space coves, scaling sequences, crossovers, and square structures.

LogoLounge's 2024 report also notes that identity may be carried by wordmarks,
motion, patterns, textures, color, and graphic systems rather than a standalone
symbol alone. It identifies saturation around AI sparkle/dazzle imagery and rapid
cycling of familiar aesthetics.

Operational conclusion:

- use trends to identify collision risk and audience expectations;
- do not combine named trends into prompts;
- do not equate current appearance with strategic fit;
- prefer an ownable invariant over a fashionable treatment;
- consider whether a strong wordmark or wider identity system should carry more
  recognition than an unnecessary abstract symbol.

## 7. AI-Generation Failure Modes

Image models optimize toward recognizable visual priors. For abstract logos they
commonly collapse into:

- letters C, S, A, V, G, or N;
- arrows, portals, links, shields, locks, eyes, wings, flowers, mountains;
- sparkle/AI symbols, chat bubbles, circuits, or app tiles;
- detached primitives with post-hoc conceptual stories;
- default geometric sans wordmarks with random stencil cuts;
- attractive presentation scale but unusable micro geometry.

Countermeasures:

1. Prompt one formal invariant and one dominant event, not an essay.
2. State the product evidence separately from the visual instruction.
3. Ban category and letter collisions explicitly, but limit the blacklist to the
   most likely readings; enormous negative lists dilute the main instruction.
4. Use image-to-image only after a human approves a structural seed.
5. Reject rather than explain a weak output.
6. After three non-converging generations, change the concept, representation, or
   model workflow instead of polishing the prompt indefinitely.
7. Never use model-rendered wordmarks as production typography. Recompose type with
   real fonts and vector editing after direction approval.

## 8. Reference-Image Policy

Do not bundle third-party trend archives or social-media screenshots into the
skill. They are narrow, age quickly, create copyright/provenance ambiguity, and
bias every project toward the same visual grammar.

Use a reference only when:

- its source and permission status are known;
- it is relevant to one declared operation or craft problem;
- the prompt explicitly prohibits copying visible marks;
- no more than two references are attached to one candidate;
- the output can be justified without the reference.

Prefer project-owned assets, public brand guidelines, type specimens, material
references, or purpose-built construction sketches over trend collages.

## 9. Review Scorecard

Score each dimension 0–2. A candidate needs at least 17/22 and cannot score zero
on product evidence, cultural/art foundation, distinctiveness, or small-size survival.

| Dimension | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Product evidence | generic/post-hoc | partially connected | mechanism visibly drives form |
| Cultural/art foundation | invented or absent | relevant but weakly constraining | verified source and named principle govern construction |
| Source distinctiveness | category cliche | some unique detail | ownable invariant |
| Silhouette | confused | recognizable | immediate and memorable |
| Counterform | accidental | serviceable | essential to identity |
| Collision risk | dominant wrong reading | manageable | low after review |
| Simplicity/specificity | generic or diagrammatic | balanced imperfectly | simple and proprietary |
| Small-size survival | fails | needs optical variant | works at 16–24 px |
| One-color survival | loses identity | mostly survives | invariant remains clear |
| Wordmark relationship | pasted/default | partially related | coherent shared grammar |
| System extensibility | one-off picture | some applications | supports motion/layout/pattern |

Also perform:

- blur test;
- mirror test;
- 16/24/32/48 px test;
- light/dark and one-color test;
- memory redraw test;
- nearest-letter and nearest-object naming test;
- category-contact-sheet collision review;
- basic trademark search before promotion, with legal review as external handoff.

## 10. Production Gates

1. **Evidence gate**: buyer sentence and unique mechanism approved.
2. **Family gate**: at least three structurally different operations.
3. **Collision gate**: wrong readings named before refinement.
4. **Symbol gate**: one candidate passes the scorecard without a wordmark.
5. **Lockup gate**: real typography, optical spacing, responsive arrangements.
6. **Robustness gate**: micro, mono, reverse, mask, and contrast checks.
7. **System gate**: color, motion, layout, imagery, and product tokens derive from
   the approved invariant rather than introducing unrelated motifs.
8. **Clearance gate**: trademark and licensing work recorded as completed or an
   explicit external handoff.
